# Age of Ruin — 1 to 300 AF

#history #age-of-ruin

> *Previous: [[Age of Empire]] · Next: [[Age of Recovery]]*

The world after the [[Age of Empire#The Great Fracturing|Fracturing]] was unrecognisable. The asteroid belt that rings the planet — visible on clear nights as a broken band of reflected light — is the rock and debris that was thrown into the upper atmosphere by the shattering of the plane and never came back down. Great stretches of the continent had been reshaped by planar energies. The elder races had spent themselves heavily closing the rifts, and the younger races had nearly been wiped out.

This is the age of consequences.

---

## The Physical World After the Fracturing

The material plane was, in the immediate aftermath, barely habitable:

- **The asteroid belt** — permanent reminder, visible every clear night
- **Reshaped coastlines** — planar energy burst through geological structure
- **Wild magic zones** — areas where the plane had not fully reknit, where arcane energy behaved unpredictably
- **Planar bleed-through points** — locations where the boundaries between the material plane and the elemental planes had been permanently weakened
- **Rubble zones** of what had been major cities, some still structurally standing, most not

The elder races who had sacrificed their numbers to seal the rifts were, in many cases, **critically reduced**. The dwarves began their long retreat. The dragon populations would not recover for centuries. The giants became far fewer than they had been.

## The Transformation of the Elves

### The Dark Elves — 10 AF
[[faith/Saints#Saint Celzala|Saint Celzala]] and their house had spent the Fracturing tending the wounded — every race, including humans who had every reason to look at an elf with hatred. The gods transformed them in recognition of that choice. Their silver-consuming curse was shaped into **something workable and beautiful**. Each member of the house was given an element of unnatural loveliness to ease the dying's fear.

Celzala themselves became the **guide of souls** — the figure who meets the dead at the boundary between the world and the Greylands.

→ See: [[nations/The Elven Isle#Celesphain Dusk City|Celesphain]]

### The Wood Elves
House Varelis had spent the Fracturing period teaching farming to communities the empire had displaced. Their bronze metal was made shapeable — the oldest wood elves learned to grow a complete metallic carapace from their own transformed bodies, protective armour for the forests they had spent centuries tending.

## Saint Bricwar — 25 AF

The giant king who led the forces that fought alongside the dwarves and dragons to seal the rifts opened by the Fracturing. **Fell in the last day of rift-sealing**, from wounds inflicted by creatures that should not have been possible to wound.

The glowing scars that mark Saint Bricwar in every depiction are real — earned in that final battle. They passed to Bricwar's descendants and remain among the most significant markers of direct giant lineage. Grand Chieftain [[nations/Nomadic Lands of the Giants|Solanna Stonewalker]] bears them today.

→ Full profile: [[faith/Saints#Saint Bricwar|Saint Bricwar]]

## Foundation of the Undead Dominion — 100 AF

The **grand liches, vampires, and wraith-lords** who had contributed to the defence of the world during the rift-sealing established their own political structure and negotiated their place in the new world. This was not an afterthought — the undead had organised themselves into the **Four Ordos** by 150 AF, each governing a different aspect of undead life and civic function.

The covenant with the living residents of their territories was developed during this period — the formal version ratified around 400 AF, but the principles in place from the founding.

## Emperor Vauldris the Golden — c.175 AF

A human who united the surviving human settlements through **a combination of genuine charisma and a willingness to use enslaved labour from other races** to build the infrastructure his empire required.

The empire is remembered in two completely incompatible ways:
1. As the thing that brought humanity back from the edge of extinction — the infrastructure Vauldris built made human survival possible
2. As the thing that did so by treating other races' lives as costs acceptable to pay

Both are accurate. The emperor was not a simple villain — the scale of the emergency was real, and their organisational genius is not in dispute. The methods used were also real, and the suffering they caused was also real.

> *"The guilt of what had been done under Vauldris's banner is still occasionally used as a rhetorical weapon in diplomatic negotiations involving human nations, three thousand years later."*

---

## The Fall of the First Human Empire — c.250 AF

The coalition that overthrew Vauldris's empire — dwarves, elves, dragons, giants, undead, and humans who had themselves been enslaved under the empire — produced **the first genuinely representative human governing structures**. The post-imperial settlements are the direct ancestors of the provincial system and the human participation in provincial governance that exists today.

The fall was not clean. The infrastructure Vauldris had built was now the infrastructure everyone depended on, and dismantling the empire without destroying its physical legacy required careful management that took decades and produced significant ongoing disputes about who owed what to whom.

## The Weight of 300 Years

What the Age of Ruin produced, beyond the obvious destruction, was a **reckoning**. The elder races had prevented extinction — barely — and the cost had been their demographic dominance. The younger races, particularly humans, had demonstrated both their fragility and their extraordinary capacity for recovery. The undead had proven themselves political actors rather than simply threats.

The world that emerged from 300 AF was **fundamentally more equal** than anything that had existed before — not because equality had been chosen, but because the catastrophe had levelled the playing field in ways no ideology could have achieved. The institutions of the Age of Recovery were built by people who had survived something that could have ended everything, and who had complicated feelings about almost everyone involved.
