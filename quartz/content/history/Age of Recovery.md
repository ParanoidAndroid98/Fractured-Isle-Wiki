# Age of Recovery — 300 to 1800 AF

#history #age-of-recovery

> *Previous: [[Age of Ruin]] · Next: [[The Modern Age]]*

The period from 300 AF onward is the long, slow, unglamorous work of **building a world that functions**. No single dramatic event defines the Age of Recovery — it is defined by the accumulation of small decisions made by people who had inherited a broken world and chose, institution by institution, agreement by agreement, to make it less broken.

This is the age that produced most of the infrastructure the modern world depends on.

---

## Institutional Reconstruction

### The Ebony Order and the Arcane Senate
Both institutions had been reduced to skeleton operations during the Fracturing and its aftermath. The rebuilding process took most of the early Recovery era and resulted in the **more structured, more formalised versions** that exist in 3000 AF. The debates over how much to expand, how strictly to regulate, and what to do with the knowledge recovered from the Fracturing's wreckage were contentious and lasting.

The Order's deep archive was substantially expanded during this period — the Fracturing had left behind a great deal of arcane material that needed to be assessed, locked away, or destroyed, and the Order was not always certain which of those three was correct.

### The Banking Infrastructure
The [[organisations/The Banks|Gold Dragon Province's banking system]] spread to every major city during the Recovery era. The **promissory note system** — the financial innovation that made large-scale trade possible without moving physical treasure — was developed and refined over this period, producing the financial stability that the modern provincial economy depends on.

## The Frozen Famine — c.800 AF

The Silver Dragon Province suffered catastrophic crop failure and near-total loss of the coastal fish harvest over a three-year period. The cause was never fully determined — possible planar residue affecting weather systems, possible magical contamination of soil and water, possibly simply bad luck extended by the province's already marginal agricultural conditions.

What happened: **the Green Dragon Province arrived with food and medicine without being asked**.

No formal request had been made. No treaty obligated the response. The Green Province's druids simply identified the crisis and mobilised. The alliance that grew from that winter is **the most stable in the Isles**, built not on strategic calculation but on the memory of specific generosity — a principle the Green Province's culture takes seriously enough to still practise.

The Silver Province's tradition of communal resource pooling, and the cultural architecture around ensuring no community member goes hungry, dates from the Frozen Famine.

## The Spirit Riots — c.400 AF

Living citizens of the Undead Lands protesting the ethics of undead labour — specifically, objecting to the conditions under which the covenant was being applied in practice, which in the early centuries had drifted from the principles negotiated at founding.

The riots were **resolved through political negotiation** rather than suppression, using the house structure the Four Ordos had been developing since the Dominion's founding. The formal covenant that now governs the relationship between the living and the undead was ratified as a direct result — a document that has been tested, challenged, and reaffirmed multiple times since, and that continues to govern one of the most unusual polities in the world.

## The TideLock Agreement — c.500 AF

A period of widespread piracy and trade disruption in the southern shipping lanes had been damaging provincial economies across the Isles for decades. Vapos's clergy mediated negotiations between the affected parties — a process that took eleven years and required financial instruments that only the [[organisations/The Banks|banking system]] could provide.

The **TideLock Agreement** stabilised the shipping lanes and established the international maritime law framework that still governs trade and passage. It has held for over a thousand years. The Agreement is sometimes cited as the clearest evidence that Vapos's clergy produce better outcomes than anyone else in mediation — three other attempts using secular mediators had failed in the preceding generation.

## The Ashen Eruption — c.900 AF

The [[nations/Red Dragon Province|Red Dragon Province]] had been warned, repeatedly and by multiple sources, that mining operations in the northern volcanic range were destabilising the geological systems. The warnings were overruled. The volcanic system destabilised.

The Ashen Eruption did not destroy the province — but it came close to destroying several major settlements and rendered a significant stretch of territory uninhabitable for decades. The reconstruction effort produced the Red Province's **current expertise in disaster response and hazard management**, knowledge that has since been shared with other provinces and credited with reducing casualties in several subsequent crises.

> *"Umeial's greatest regret, by their own account, was approving the deeper mining of the northern volcanic range. The engineer who objected was overruled."*

## Famous Figures of the Recovery

### Alborak SteelFist — c.600 AF
A dwarven figure whose most famous act — the defeat of a dragon in single combat — is disputed in every detail except the outcome. The dragon is demonstrably dead. How exactly it died depends on who is telling the story and what they think about dwarves. The event prompted the first formal negotiations between dwarven communities and the dragon provinces over trade routes through the mountain territories.

### Corvin Black Feather — c.700 AF
An aarakocra explorer who proved the world was spherical through a twenty-three-year circumnavigation — the first fully documented one. The mathematical hypothesis had existed for centuries; Black Feather provided the empirical evidence. The [[organisations/Pathfinders of Aestri|Pathfinders]] claim descent from their tradition, though the formal organisation wouldn't be founded for another eleven hundred years.

### Captain Seraph Tideborn — c.1200 AF
The first person to survive a close approach to the [[locations/Landmark Locations#The Sunken Maw|Sunken Maw]] and return with usable data. Their maps of the Maw's behaviour patterns — tidal cycles, creature activity, the sound it produces at specific intervals — are still the primary source for anyone studying the location.

### The Wayfinder — 500 AF to present *(?)* 
A recurring figure in traveller accounts across the entire Recovery era and into the modern age — always a changeling, always appearing at geographical crossroads at moments of crisis, always pointing the lost toward safety. Whether the Wayfinder is one person, several people, or something else entirely is one of the most enjoyable unsolved mysteries in the Pathfinders' archive.

## The Relaxation Rebellion — c.850 AF

The bath house and hot spring workers of the [[nations/Gold Dragon Province|Gold Dragon Province]] went on strike over working conditions, pay structures, and the province's treatment of workers in the entertainment and hospitality industries. The rebellion was resolved through mediation and produced **the worker protections that are now the basis of labour law across three provinces**.

The Relaxation Rebellion is a favourite example among labour historians of how apparently frivolous industrial disputes can produce systemic legal changes — and among political philosophers of how the Gold Province's culture of wealth performance creates genuine material harms that eventually have to be addressed.
